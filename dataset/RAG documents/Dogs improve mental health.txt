Dogs improve mental health by reducing stress, anxiety, and depression while increasing feelings of companionship and purpose. Interacting with dogs lowers cortisol (the stress hormone) and boosts feel-good chemicals like oxytocin and dopamine. They provide unconditional love, encourage daily physical activity, and establish routines that foster better emotional well-being. 
Stress and Anxiety Reduction: Petting a dog significantly lowers stress, with studies showing an increase in feel-good hormones and a reduction in blood pressure.
Companionship and Reduced Loneliness: Dogs offer consistent, non-judgmental support, which helps alleviate feelings of isolation and loneliness.
Physical Activity and Routine: Regular, required walks with dogs increase exercise, which boosts endorphins, while feeding and care schedules provide crucial daily structure.
Social Connection: Walking a dog often sparks conversations with others, enhancing social interaction and helping individuals connect with their community.
Purpose and Self-Esteem: Caring for a dog gives owners a sense of purpose and responsibility, which can improve self-worth and provide a reason to get out of bed, especially for those with depression.
Support for Specific Conditions: Dogs are known to help with PTSD symptoms, with a high percentage of owners reporting reduced symptoms and better emotional management. 
These benefits are rooted in a "biopsychosocial framework," where the physical, emotional, and social impacts of dog ownership combine to improve overall mental health.
